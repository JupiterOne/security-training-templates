The Basics

  • What is HIPAA?

    HIPAA stands for Health Insurance Portability & Accountability Act of 1996 (45 C.F.R. parts 160 & 164)

  • What is it for?

    It is a nationwide framework for protection of patient confidentiality, security of electronic systems, and standards and requirements for electronic transmission of health information.

  • Key parts of HIPAA include:

    - HIPAA Security Rule
    - HIPAA Privacy Rule
    - Breach Notification

What is PHI and ePHI?

Any data that is individually identifiable health information is considered Protected Health Information (PHI). ePHI is PHI in electronic form.

In 2013, the HIPAA Omnibus Rule amended HIPAA to include genetic information in the definition of PHI

This includes:

- Names
- Account numbers
- Geographic data
- Certificate/license numbers
- All elements of dates
- Vehicle identifiers, serial numbers, license plates
- Telephone numbers
- Device identifiers and serial numbers
- FAX numbers
- Web URLs
- Email addresses
- Internet protocol addresses
- Social Security numbers
- Biometric identifiers (i.e. retinal scan, fingerprints)
- Medical record numbers
- Full face photos and comparable images
- Health plan beneficiary numbers
- Any unique identifying number, characteristic or code

Privacy Rule

HIPAA Privacy Rule went into effect April 14, 2003.

The Privacy Rule

  • Establishes national standards to protect individuals’ medical records and other PHI.
  • Defines how patient information used and disclosed.
  • Gives patients privacy rights and more control over their own health information.

Security Rule

HIPAA Security Rule went into effect April 21, 2005.

The Security Rule

  • Establishes national standards to protect how individuals’ ePHI is created, received, used, or maintained.
  • Requires the following safeguards to ensure of the Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA) of ePHI:
    • Administrative Safeguards
    • Physical Safeguards
    • Technical Safeguards

Covered Entities

 A Covered Entity is one of the following:

  • Healthcare Provider, such as doctors, clinics, pharmacies, who transmits any health information in electronic form;
  • Health Plan, such as health insurance companies; or
  • Heathcare Clearinghouse, such as billing services and health information systems.

Business Associates

 A Business Associate is

  • Any person or entity that performs certain functions or activities that involve the use or disclosure of protected health information on behalf of, or provides services to, a covered entity.

{{company}} is a covered entity and may also be a business associate to another covered entity.

Business Associate Agreement (BAA)

A Business Associate Agreement (BAA) must be signed by any business entity that may have access to our PHI or ePHI, prior to conducting business with {{company}}, including Vendors, Partners, and Customers.

BAA with a vendor is required when
  • Vendor have to use or disclose PHI to perform its service; or
  • Vendor have unmonitored access to PHI, regardless of its intended use.
     
BAA with a vendor is NOT required when
  • Vendor has no access to PHI; or
  • Access to PHI is NOT required for the vendor to perform its service AND the access to PHI is monitored.

HITECH and the Omnibus Final Rule

  • Under Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health (HITECH) Act of 2009, business associates are held to the same standards as covered entities to comply with HIPAA Privacy Rule and Security Rule
  • HIPAA Omnibus Rule implements statutory amendments under HITECH and further strengthens the privacy and security protection of PHI
  • HITECH and the Omnibus Rule also requires HIPAA covered entities and their business associates to provide notification following a breach of unsecured protected health information

Patient Rights, Disclosure and Release

  • Individuals have the right to keep their health information private whether it is stored on paper or electronically.
  • {{company}} provides a written Notice of Privacy Practices, explaining how health information is kept private, the usage and disclosures of PHI, and the process to access data or to file a compliant, available at https://{{company}}.com/privacy.
  • In general, PHI can be disclosed, without authorization, to a public health authority (e.g. FDA, CDC, OSHA) for purposes like monitoring disease or injury; reporting vital events such as births or deaths; and conducting any kind of public health surveillance, like investigations or interventions.
  • Patient authorization is not necessary if a disclosure is made for purposes of treatment, securing payment, or in accordance with the operations of a health care provider. If PHI is to be disclosed for other purposes, the patient's written authorization is mandatory.

Data Protection & Breach Notification

Remember the following do’s and don’ts when handling ePHI:

DO
  • ePHI must be encrypted when stored and transmitted
  • Require a signed BAA with any third party that may have access to our PHI or ePHI – the {{company}} BAA template is stored on company SharePoint
  • Production data, including audit logs, must be retained for a minimum of 7 years
  • We are obligated to report a breach of unsecured (e.g. unencrypted) PHI to affected individuals, the Dept. of Health and Human Services (HHS), and in some case, the media. Breach notification must be handled by approval personnel only.
DON'T
  • Do not discuss patient information in public or post ePHI to public forums
  • Never exchange ePHI via email, chat, or SMS text messages Note: Slack is NOT HIPAA compliant, therefore no PHI may be exchanged on any channel, direct messages, screen sharing, or discussed via Slack calls.
  • Do not store ePHI locally on end-user computing devices, including laptops, tablets and smartphones, or removable media such as USB drives
  • Do not use non-BAA eligible services in our production environments, such as Amazon Web Services (AWS) accounts, to store, process, and transmit ePHI

Enforcement, Violations & Penalties

HIPAA is a mandatory regulation that is enforced and audited by the Office for Civil Rights (OCR) of the Dept. of Health & Human Services (HHS).

Failure to comply may result in significant penalty, including
  • fines of up to $1.5 million annually, especially due to willful neglect; and
  • criminal prosecution for “knowingly” misuse of PHI or the sale/transfer of PHI for commercial or personal gain or other monetary or malicious purpose.

Note these recent events:
  • There has been over 1,800 breaches between 2009 and 2016 leaving more than 171,000,000 healthcare records and personal data exposed
    Source: http://www.hipaajournal.com/largest-healthcare-data-breaches-of-2016-8631/
  • The OCR imposed close to $23M of fines in 2016 alone
